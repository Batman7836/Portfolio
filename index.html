Final Version

Ali Rezaee & MohammadJavad Ashrafi present: Identity

Identity is made of many different aspects, each of which contributes to its meaning and importance in our lives.

First and foremost is the place where we’re born, our country of origin. Our nationality dictates the culture and language we grow up with, which in turn forms our overall identity. For instance, our culture may require us to follow a certain dress code that will become a part of our identity. Language also plays a crucial role in shaping our thoughts and the way we communicate with others, reinforcing our cultural roots.

Another characteristic that forms a part of our identity is race. Some people may view and treat us differently based on the color of our skin. For example, the African-American community in the US has its own subculture. Race can also impact our experiences, opportunities, and even our interactions with society, sometimes leading to social inequality.

Family is one of the other factors that influence a person's identity. Once it comes to talking about what forms the identity of an individual, the way their family treats them and things they learn from them is—in our opinion—the most important thing that you can refer to.

Gender also plays a role in shaping identity. Society often assigns different expectations based on gender, influencing our roles and opportunities. In many cultures, traditional gender norms dictate the kind of job you will have, what you can and cannot wear, the places you can go to and the activities you can participate in. For example, in certain cultures women are expected to be tradwives, limiting them to doing house chores and never allowing them to explore this world. These women are treated as objects, never to have an identity of their own.

Age influences identity as well. As we get older and more mature, our worldview also changes. As children we are too innocent and pure to see the realities of our society, but as we enter adulthood our identity changes in order to adapt to the society we now have to live in.

Finally, identity is deeply connected to society, the environment in which we have to live in until our hair turns the same color as our teeth. The government dictates laws that people have to follow, and that shapes our identity in a way that is really out of our control.

All in all, there are lots of different factors that have a hand in forming the identity of a person, including but not limited to family, nationality, race, gender, and age, with each one affecting the creation of our identity in a special way.

This piece was mine and Mr. Ashrafi’s very first, it was a great collaboration with my dear friend. Free writing, as the name suggests, doesn’t require that many instructions or pre-existing knowledge, we only need to write down exactly what comes to mind about a certain topic on a piece of paper. I believe that is why this was the first lesson to be taught in this course. It was a good first step that helped us get past the feeling of having to be perfect and just get our real thoughts down. The refining process introduced us to the idea of multiple drafts for a single text. Perhaps the goal of this exercise was to evaluate how good our writing skill was at the beginning of the semester, which gave us a starting point to see how much we improved.

Working on this text taught us many valuable skills. The most important one was perhaps learning how to use MindMup, a platform we would use to brainstorm and make mind maps with for the rest of the semester. This tool proved to be very helpful. I personally found it much easier to organize my thoughts with this platform, which in turn helped me to write better texts. Seeing my ideas laid out like that made it easier to see how they connected, which helped me structure my writing better than just using a normal list.

In my first attempt at free writing I failed to deliver a truly ‘free’ text. My brain has gotten used to formatting text, having a introductory sentence, a body and a conclusion, so I couldn’t really ignore all that and write a ‘free’ text. This showed me that it was hard to break away from the usual rules of writing I had learned over the years and try something more creative.

In my second attempt, however, I did manage to write something that was closer to a free writing. I ignored most writing rules and just produced on paper what was in my mind at the time. It was a good lesson in not worrying about being perfect right from the start. These writings are unfortunately lost due to them being hand-written.

We then used our original free writings to create a mind map on paper which we would then use to write our finalized text. The mind map consisted of the ideas we had unconsciously embedded in our texts. This step was really important because it helped us turn our first messy ideas into the organized essay we needed to write.

The mind map then helped us write our first organized essay which was about Identity. I along with my partner wrote certain parts of the text, making sure to use as many of our ideas as possible. Later we refined our text and submitted it to the google docs space for all to view.

In conclusion, this lesson helped me understand the process of brainstorming, the importance of mind maps, and of course how to collaborate on a piece with a peer. It really showed me how writing is a process with many steps, and that a good piece of writing doesn't just happen all at once, but comes from thinking, working together, and making changes along the way.

Final Version

Responsibility for the Spread of Misinformation

We are currently living in the age of ICT (Information and Communication Technology), an age dominated by the rise of social media platforms. With the invention of the Internet, our world has become more interconnected than ever. Information can be transferred from one corner of our globe to another in mere seconds. Though this technology has allowed us to grow and develop as a society, giving us useful information that we would have otherwise never been able to access, it has also caused much harmful and false information to spread around the world like wildfire. This phenomenon has posed a serious question: should social media platforms be held responsible for the spread of misinformation? I believe they should be, since they are the ones designing the systems that encourage its rapid spread. Misinformation has real-world consequences, and they have the technological capacity to better moderate their massive platforms.

One argument that is often used to lift the blame from social media platforms is that they provide us with a really large quantity of useful information, from global news to scientific content and lots of other useful content such as guides, etc. They have enabled us to share knowledge across borders like never before. While it’s true that a lot of valuable information exists on these platforms, we still cannot ignore the effects of misinformation on their users. Studies have shown that false and inaccurate information has a tendency to spread much faster and reach a larger portion of said users. These users often consider the misinformation as fact, and their actions reflect that. For example, during the Covid-19 pandemic, a lot of false information regarding vaccines gained popularity on social media platforms. Many conspiracy theories started to appear, falsely claiming that vaccines contained microchips, altered the human DNA, or caused severe health complications. This led to many people not vaccinating and losing their lives.

Another argument that is usually brought up is that social media platforms are already doing their best to stop misinformation from spreading, and thus they shouldn’t get the blame for it. This is a very bold claim, as we all know, all these platforms really care about is ad revenue and user engagement. Their algorithms will promote anything that will get the users to stay online for long periods of time. That content doesn’t always get fact-checked and filtered by their pathetically small moderation teams. It is clear that the priority for these platforms is not the accuracy of the information being shared but rather the level of engagement and interaction it can generate. This means that controversial content, often misinformation—spreads more than factual and reliable content. The more time users spend on the platform, the more advertisements they see, and the more revenue the platform generates.

Finally, some argue that individuals are responsible for what they choose to believe in and that people shouldn’t simply believe every bit of information that they come across on the internet. It is true that people should be really careful about believing online data and information. However, when the algorithm is heavily promoting a certain agenda and actively feeding the users misinformation over and over again, how can people tell the difference between false and true information? The human brain is hardcoded to look for information that confirms our preexisting beliefs, a concept known as confirmation bias. When social media algorithms keep on pushing content that aligns with a user’s previous interactions, it reinforces their existing views, making it even harder for individuals to differentiate between fact and fiction.

Misinformation on the internet has the potential to cause real-world damage. As discussed before, when people take false information as fact, they tend to base their actions upon it. The anti-vax movement during the Covid-19 pandemic was a perfect example of that. Another example can be the Rohingya genocide in Burma. In 2016, as the Burmese people were rapidly connecting to the internet, they were greeted by a wave of new information on platforms such as Facebook, and of course, some of that information, especially regarding the Rohingya people, was false. The Burmese people started to view the Rohingya very negatively and carried out a brutal genocide. These are only a few examples of the countless real-world tragedies that were caused by misinformation on social media.

Social media companies are more than capable of suppressing misinformation on their platforms. With millions of dollars in revenue, they could invest in robust moderation teams that focus on combating misinformation. But the fact of the matter is, they choose not to do this because it damages their revenue. Users are more interested in content that intrigues them, and misinformation does exactly that. If these companies were required by law to spend more on moderation, the amount of misinformation on their platforms would decrease significantly.

To conclude, in this day and age, people spend a decent amount of their time scrolling through social media. They don’t always have the time or resources to research every bit of information they see on these platforms. Oftentimes, they take them as facts, and this can indeed be very dangerous. If we unite against this storm of misinformation, we can combat it properly and prevent the horrible consequences of its spread. With proper laws and regulations, increased moderation efforts, and greater awareness, we can create an online space where truth prevails over lies.

A Description of Guernica

This seems to me like a series of statues that have been destroyed by a blast, probably as a result of the bombings. To the left, the bull stands as an important animal to the Spanish; they have rituals where their Matadors face off against bulls or where the bull chases the crowd through the streets. The bull is a sort of antagonist, a symbol of evil within Spanish culture, and those who dare to challenge it are regarded as heroes. Crumbling under the bull, I see a woman and her dead child as she cries in agony. Did her child die at the hands of the bull? Perhaps the bull represents the people who sided with the fascists in the war, those who betrayed their own people.

In the center of the painting, I see a horse stepping over the body of a fallen soldier, and it appears to be mourning the death of the hero. The horse is often the symbol of freedom, so we could see this as “freedom weeps as heroes die.” The freedom fighter dying is portrayed here as a horse losing its rider.

To the right of the picture, a building is on fire, with people burning inside and screaming, crying out for help. On the bottom, a woman can be seen desperately trying to escape the burning building; she appears to be reaching for freedom. Another woman cannot even attempt to escape. She is a victim of war, another number added to the sum of casualties. No one is there to save her; no one hears her cries.

From a window above, I see a person peeking their head out, holding what appears to be a lamp. Perhaps she is the symbol of the observer, those who wish to sit out the war and look from a distance. But she's also in danger, showing that not even the neutrals can escape the horrors of war.

At last, there is the shining beacon at the top center of the painting. Perhaps this is a beacon of hope, or maybe the light from the blast... I belive the point with these surreal paintings is that people can interpret them in different ways and give them meanings of their own. One might see the light as a sign of hope, while others might view it as a sign of iminent death. In the end, one thing is clear: “War is not hell. War is war, and hell is hell. And of the two of them, war is a lot worse.”

Argumentative Essay: AI in Academia

Final Version

Should university students be allowed to use A.I. for their assignments?

Artificial Intelligence’s (AI’s) rise in many different fields can’t be ignored. This has caused heated arguments among the experts of said fields. Ever since AI tools like ChatGPT and GrammarlyAI became common for students, the field of education has suffered a massive decline. This brings up a key question. Should university students use AI for their academic work? AI offers benefits like efficiency and support; however, it also raises concerns about plagiarism. Is using AI cheating? This essay will argue that AI shouldn't be banned in academics; instead, its use needs regulation and monitoring. This ensures AI doesn't hurt students' learning.

Despite its many benefits, there are concerns about overreliance on AI. The aim of education in general is to encourage independent thought and inspire new ideas. It also teaches us how to solve problems and grow as a person. If students use AI for whole assignments, they might not learn anything. Al-Abdullatif and Sereetrakul (2023) note that AI overdependence can reduce the students’ critical thinking and lessen their personal accountability. All these are valid reasons to be concerned; however, with proper regulations in place to limit the usage of AI in academia, we can prevent this overreliance. For example, students may be allowed only a few AI prompts per assignment, so they will learn to use it wisely and get the best out of it.

Professors might also find grading student work difficult. It can prove difficult to tell AI writing from human writing, which could cause grade inflation or unfair grades. Students who do their work independently might feel demotivated and resort to using AI themselves. Others using AI might get similar grades with less effort (Marr, 2023). Professors are right to be concerned about the abundance of AI-generated content being passed off as human-written. Although, with many robust AI detection tools available online, the task of recognizing AI content has become easier than ever.

Plagiarism is another major concern. AI-generated content can seem original, especially if the student attempts to refine it, but it's trained on existing essays and materials. Much of this source material is plagiarized and taken without the consent of their authors. Dishonest AI use could violate academic honesty and codes of ethics. The World Intellectual Property Organization (2023) states generative AI creates many ethical issues within the context of academia. As mentioned before, the many AI detectors available online can also check for AI plagiarism, thus eliminating this concern for good.

The last issue, which is perhaps the most important, is creativity. Academic writing is not just the transfer of information, especially in fields like humanities and social sciences. It involves developing a voice of our own, making original arguments, and expressing our unbiased views. Outsourcing these tasks to AI means students may miss crucial chances to build their own intellectual identity. This is perhaps the most serious issue regarding AI, but there's a simple solution for it: Limit AI to labor-intensive tasks and leave the creative tasks for humans. AI can generate summaries, take notes, etc., while the students focus on the important and creative tasks.

AI's main advantage is improving student productivity. AI tools can automate tasks. These include generating summaries, formatting references, or checking grammar, which saves students a lot of time. An Inside Higher Ed report (2023) showed over 60% of students felt AI helped them to some extent: They managed their workloads better and felt less stressed. AI acts as our academic assistant, letting us focus on complex tasks which require critical thinking and creativity.

Knowing how to use AI is also a vital 21st-century skill. Crafting good prompts is important for most tasks. Analyzing AI content is now known by many as "prompt engineering." The tech industry already recognizes this practice, and some even believe it should be a university major. Morley and Floridi (2023) suggest AI use in academia builds technical skills that are essential for our future careers. Learning responsible AI use can open more job options for us and also prepares students for their future job markets.

A balanced approach is therefore necessary. Universities shouldn't ban AI entirely; they also shouldn't allow unrestricted use. Instead, they should set clear rules. These rules should guide ethical and productive AI use. As mentioned previously, AI could be allowed for doing most non-creative, time-consuming tasks. Examples include citation management, proofreading, or outlining.

Educators must clearly state AI use policies. They should specify if AI is allowed and under what conditions should students use it. Transparency is absolutely crucial from both academic institutions and their students. Disclosing AI help is sort of like citing sources; this can help maintain academic decency.

In conclusion, AI can improve our academic experience, but its unregulated use can harm our educational goals. University students should learn to use AI tools responsibly and ethically. Higher education aims to create independent thinkers; it seeks to foster creative and critical minds, not just good assignments and high grades. If used well, AI can and will support this mission, not stop it.

Original Draft

The recent widespread arrival of artificial intelligence (AI) has stoked endless controversy across most sectors, particularly the academic one. With AI tools such as ChatGPT, GrammarlyAI, and Notion AI being accessible to students, the question at hand is: should university students use AI for assignments? While AI presents numerous advantages in terms of productivity and academic support, it also raises issues of academic integrity, learning experience, and ethics on the flip side. This essay argues that while AI should not be entirely excluded from academic settings, its use needs to be balanced and morally established to preserve the inherent functions of education.

The primary advantage of AI is that it can increase academic productivity. AI tools can facilitate activities like summarizing, organizing references, or correcting grammar, freeing up scholars with plenty of time. According to research by Inside Higher Ed (2023), over 60% of the students polled affirmed that they used AI to better manage their academic load. Here, AI is taking the form of an academic helper, allowing students to pursue more deeply involved work involving critical thinking and creativity.

In addition, mastery of AI is becoming a critical 21st-century skill. Having the ability to design effective prompts and interpret AI-produced text is a form of "prompt engineering," a practice already being performed in the tech industry. In alignment with the suggestion of Morley and Floridi (2023), incorporating AI tools into scholarly assignments provides students with the digital literacy competencies they will most likely possess in upcoming professional life. Therefore, learning how to interact with AI responsibly may enhance employability and prepare students for a rapidly changing technological landscape.

However, apart from these benefits, there are serious concerns regarding the overuse of AI by students. One of the prime purposes of education is to facilitate independent thinking, problem-solving, and self-growth. When students use AI to do entire assignments, they may lose the whole learning process. Al-Abdullatif and Sereetrakul (2023) state that excessive dependence on AI tools can lead to weaker critical thinking and lack of personal accountability in work.

Teachers also have to measure student performance to some extent. AI-generated writing is increasingly hard to differentiate from human work, and it is challenging to know whether a student has indeed read course material. This may cause grades to be inflated or unequally benefit students academically. Students who perform their own work may also be demotivated when peers who utilize AI receive the same grades with much less effort (Marr, 2023).

Academic integrity is also of primary concern. AI-generated work, though original in outcome, is trained on enormous databases, many of which contain copyrighted or plagiarized material. As such, nondisclosure use of AI can inadvertently breach principles of academic integrity and intellectual property. As the World Intellectual Property Organization (2023) observes, generative AI poses moral questions regarding copyright and authorship of creative content, especially when works by authors and artists are utilized to train such tools without authorization.

There is creativity as well. It's not only about reporting information in the humanities and social sciences, it is about constructing a voice, an argument, and expressing a detailed perspective. When students delegate these tasks to AI, they risk missing an opportunity to develop their own unique intellectual voice.

In recognition of these concerns, a balanced approach is warranted. Rather than an outright ban of AI or unrestricted use, universities need to have clearly defined policies regarding how AI applications can be used ethically and positively as part of academic work. For example, AI can be permitted for mundane, time-consuming tasks such as citation management, proofreading, or outlining. Critical thinking, originality, and personal analysis-based tasks, however, need to be accomplished without AI assistance.

Furthermore, instructors need to clearly state whether the employment of AI is allowed in assignments and on what terms. Institutions and students alike should be open. Revealing help from AI, similar to revealing a tutor or other external aid, can maintain academic honesty without stifling creativity.

Lastly, while AI can make the learning experience richer, uncontrolled use can undermine educational goals. University students must be prodded to employ AI tools responsibly and ethically. The mission of higher education is not merely to produce assignments, but to produce independent, innovative, and critical thinkers. Employed properly, AI can help advance that mission, not replace it.
